Susannah Locke

Susannah is currently an associate editor. Before that, she was a science reporter here and an editor over at Popular Science magazine.

For Vox.com, she focuses on bringing reporters, designers, and web developers together to create robust, multimedia storytelling experiences. She also works to make new tools for the Vox.com editorial team.

What do advice do you have for aspiring young storytellers?

Question:Garfield began as a comic strip and over the years has evolved into a story told across many platforms, including television, film, books, and more. How has your thinking about the Garfield character changed to accommodate a multimedia approach?

Answer:My thinking about the Garfield character has not changed to accommodate the different media; rather I’ve learned more about Garfield’s character thanks engaging him with the different media.

In the comic strip, I have to tell a story in 25 words or less. The storylines are relatively simple and straight-forward.

In a book, I have more space and time to tell a story than in a comic strip; for TV, I have a half hour, and for a movie, I can take a luxurious hour and a half to tell a story. Garfield is a more complicated character in the longer stories. He has to dig deeper and work harder to support the longer and more challenging story arcs, and that’s a good thing.

Next to the comic strip, I would say that animation is my favorite vehicle for creating great humor. In animation, I get more tools to work with like voice, music and sound effects.

Question:In the early days, how did you come up with ideas for Garfield’s story?

Answer:I still write for Garfield the same way. It’s like meditation. I focus on Garfield until I can see him in my head and then I present him with a situation like climbing a tree and then I just sit back and watch. When he does something funny, I back up three frames and cut it off. In essence, Garfield writes the material; I just edit.
