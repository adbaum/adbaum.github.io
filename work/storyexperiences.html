Story Experiences

Not every story can be told with paragraph after paragraph.

The ways people write or speak can cause a story to fall flat or lead audiences through a journey.

The way people design stories is similar. Design should support a narrative, falling away when necessary and commanding attention when appropriate.

As a member of Vox's Product team, I've been an expert in storytelling, creating things as small as simple illustrations and as complex as alternative storyforms.

Though much of the work I did was story-specific, it influenced future design of similar content types.

For instance, this piece I worked on for vox.com with Uy Tieu and Yuri Victor required an alternative to a photo gallery that would allow for a more fluid photo-based form.

This format has since been reused for other photo essays on Vox.com.

Other pieces have taken completely alternative forms, but have successfully conveyed points or supplemented larger narratives. This is a piece I worked on with Josh Laincz for The Verge. It was meant to be simple and highly shareable.

In some instances, I'm able to execute my designs in code. This was the case for a silly dessert-mashup generator that Dylan Lathrop illustrated.

And then some pieces combine a little bit of everything.

For instance, I worked with developer Aidan Feay to create the following piece, which combines an illustrative animation with a thoughtful type treatment to set a scene. It also incorporates a storymap, helping users under a complex series of events.

With every story I've made, I've kept in mind the larger context. Each unique experience can be stripped to its most important elements – both in code and in design – to create new and reusable storyforms in different contexts.
